## The Passport: What Is a Certificate?

When you travel abroad, you need a **passport**. The passport proves your identity, and it's issued by an authority that other countries trust.

In the digital world, a **certificate** works exactly the same way.

<View>
![Digital Certificate](./diagram-digital-certificate.png)
</View>

<Speaker voice="narrator">
A certificate says: "I am who I claim to be, and here's the proof."
</Speaker>

### The Three Key Elements

| Element | Explanation | Passport Analogy |
|---------|-------------|------------------|
| **Identity** | Who the certificate belongs to | Your name and photo |
| **Public Key** | A mathematical value for verification | Your passport number |
| **Signature** | Proof from a trusted authority | The official stamp |

<SoundEffect name="success-chime" />

## Certificate Authorities: Who Issues the Passports?

<Speaker voice="host" name="Host">
You can't just issue your own passport. You need an authority. The same goes for certificates.
</Speaker>

A **Certificate Authority** (CA) is a trusted third party that verifies identities and issues certificates.

<View>
![Certificate Authority Hierarchy](./diagram-ca-hierarchy.png)
</View>

Your browser comes with a list of **trusted Root CAs**. When you visit a website with HTTPS, the browser checks:

1. Is the certificate issued by a trusted CA?
2. Is it still valid (not expired)?
3. Does the domain name match?

If something fails, you see the famous **"Your connection is not private"** warning.

<Pause duration={1} />

## Code Signing: Software's Stamp

Certificates aren't just used for websites. They're also used to **sign software**.

<Speaker voice="narrator">
When you download an app, you trust that it comes from the developer. But how does your computer know?
</Speaker>

The answer is **code signing**:

1. The developer gets a certificate from a trusted CA
2. The developer signs the software with their private key
3. When you install, your computer verifies the signature

<View>
![Code Signing Process](./diagram-code-signing-flow.png)
</View>

<SoundEffect name="keyboard-typing" />

Without signing? Your computer will warn you about an "unknown developer" or block the installation entirely.

## <Voice text="S-BOM">SBOM</Voice>: Software's Ingredient List

<Speaker voice="host" name="Host">
Now we're getting to something really important â€” and something most people have never heard of.
</Speaker>

<Pause duration={1} />

Think about a ready-made meal from the supermarket. On the back of the package, you'll find an **ingredient list**:

- Flour
- Sugar
- Eggs
- Preservative E211

If you're allergic to something, you can check the list.

**<Voice text="S-BOM">SBOM</Voice>** (Software Bill of Materials) is the **ingredient list for software**.

<View>
![Software Bill of Materials (SBOM)](./diagram-sbom-ingredients.png)
</View>

### Why Is <Voice text="S-BOM">SBOM</Voice> Important?

<Speaker voice="narrator">
In December 2021, the Log4Shell vulnerability hit the world like a bomb.
</Speaker>

**Log4j** was a small logging library that almost nobody knew about. But it was hidden inside **millions** of applications:

- Minecraft
- Amazon Web Services
- Apple iCloud
- Tesla's systems

<SoundEffect name="dramatic-reveal" />

The problem? Most companies had no idea if they used Log4j. They had no ingredient list.

With an <Voice text="S-BOM">SBOM</Voice>, you can:

- **Check if you're vulnerable** when a new vulnerability is discovered
- **Trace the supply chain** back to the source
- **Prove compliance** to regulators and customers

> **"You can't protect what you don't know you have."**
