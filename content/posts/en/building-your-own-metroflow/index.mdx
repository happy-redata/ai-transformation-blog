## Before You Start: Choose Your First Process

Not everything is suited for MetroFlow. Start with something that:

‚úÖ Involves **2-4 departments** (enough complexity, but not too much)  
‚úÖ Has **5-15 steps** (manageable size)  
‚úÖ **Runs regularly** (so you can test if the map fits)  
‚úÖ **Causes pain** (something that often fails or gets delayed)

**Good first processes:**
- Vacation request approval
- New employee onboarding
- Customer inquiry handling
- Equipment purchase

**Bad first processes:**
- The entire company's annual cycle
- "Everything IT does"
- Something that only happens once a year

The first step is identifying your "lines" ‚Äî the departments or workflows involved.

### How to Do It:

1. Write all involved teams on sticky notes
2. Give each a color name (Sales = Red, Finance = Blue, etc.)

## Step 2: Plot Your Stations

Now list every single step in the process ‚Äî without editing.

### How to Do It:

1. Write each step on a sticky note
2. Mark which line (color) the step belongs to
3. Lay them out in sequence (roughly ‚Äî we'll fix the layout later)

### Example: Purchase Approval

**Blue Line (Requester):**
- Station 1: Identify need
- Station 2: Fill out requisition

**Green Line (Department):**
- Station 3: Department manager approves

**Orange Line (Procurement):**
- Station 4: Receive requisition
- Station 5: Find supplier
- Station 6: Get quotes
- Station 7: Place order

**Red Line (Finance):**
- Station 8: Receive invoice
- Station 9: Match with order
- Station 10: Pay supplier

Ten stations. You can already see the structure.

## Step 3: Mark Your Interchanges

Where does responsibility change? Those are your interchanges.

### How to Do It:

1. Find every point where work goes from one color to another
2. Mark that point as an interchange (large circle)
3. Give the interchange a name

### Example: Purchase Approval

- **Interchange A:** Between Station 2 (Blue) and Station 3 (Green) ‚Äî *"For Approval"*
- **Interchange B:** Between Station 3 (Green) and Station 4 (Orange) ‚Äî *"Approved Requisition"*
- **Interchange C:** Between Station 7 (Orange) and Station 8 (Red) ‚Äî *"Invoice Received"*

Three interchanges. Three risk points.

## Step 4: Apply the Beck Rules

Now take your chaotic layout and force it into geometry.

### Rule 1: Only 45¬∞, 90¬∞, 0¬∞
Draw a grid (10x10 works well). All lines must follow the grid lines or diagonals.

### Rule 2: Stations on Grid Points
Every station lands on an intersection in the grid. No "approximately" placements.

### Rule 3: Equal Distances
Whether the step takes 5 minutes or 5 days, the visual distance is the same.

### Rule 4: Large Circles for Interchanges
Regular stations = small tick marks. Interchanges = large white circles.

### Tools You Can Use:

| Tool | Level | Price |
|------|-------|-------|
| **Pen and paper** | Beginner | Free |
| **Miro / FigJam** | Intermediate | Freemium |
| **Figma** | Advanced | Freemium |
| **draw.io** | All levels | Free |

Start with paper. Move to digital when you're happy with the structure.

## Step 5: Share and Iterate

Here's the truth: **Your first map will be wrong.**

And that's *exactly* the point.

### How to Do It:

1. **Print the map large** (A3 or bigger)
2. **Hang it on a visible wall**
3. **Invite colleagues to comment:**
   - "Is a step missing?"
   - "Is the sequence right?"
   - "Who actually has responsibility here?"
4. **Revise based on feedback**
5. **Repeat until the map matches reality**

Most processes require 2-3 iterations before the map reflects how things actually work.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### ‚ùå Too Much Detail
MetroFlow shows flow, not procedure. Put "sub-stations" in an appendix.

### ‚ùå Ignoring Interchanges
If you only have regular stations, you've missed the point. Find the handovers.

### ‚ùå Artificial Lines
If you have 10 lines, you have too many. Consolidate where you can.

### ‚ùå Never Updating
Processes change. The map should be updated at least once a year.
