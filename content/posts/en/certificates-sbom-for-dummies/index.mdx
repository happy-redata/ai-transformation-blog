## The Invisible Web of Trust

Every time you open an app, download an update, or visit a website, you rely on an invisible network of **digital certificates**.

<View>See the illustration below ‚Äî from you to the server, data passes through many layers of trust:</View>

![The trust network ‚Äî from user to server through certificate-verified connections](/images/trust-chain-diagram.png)

Certificates are the software's **passports**. They prove:
- **Who** made the software
- **That** the software has not been changed during transit
- **When** the certificate expires

## What Is a Certificate?

<Speaker voice="narrator">
A digital certificate is like a notarized document. A trusted third party ‚Äî a <Voice text="Certificate Authority">Certificate Authority</Voice> or <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice> ‚Äî confirms that the owner is who they say they are.
</Speaker>

<Pause duration={1} />

| Element | Function |
|---------|----------|
| **Owner's identity** | Who the certificate belongs to (company, domain, person) |
| **Public key** | Used to verify signatures |
| **Issuer's signature** | The <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice> confirms the validity |
| **Expiration date** | Certificates are only valid for a limited period |

<SoundEffect name="keyboard-typing" />

When you visit a secure website and see the padlock in the browser bar, the browser has verified the website's certificate against a list of trusted <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice>s.

## The Trust Chain in Certificates

Certificates form a **chain** ‚Äî just like the trust chains we discussed in episode 1.

<View>Here's how the trust chain works:</View>

1. **Root <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice>** ‚Äî The top authority, built into your operating system
2. **Intermediate <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice>** ‚Äî Issues certificates on behalf of the Root <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice>
3. **Server Certificate** ‚Äî The specific certificate for the website or software

> **"If just one link in the chain is compromised, the entire trust collapses."**

<Pause duration={1} />

## When Certificates Fail

<Speaker voice="host" name="Host">
Certificates are not infallible. Here's what can go wrong:
</Speaker>

<SoundEffect name="dramatic-reveal" />

| Problem | Consequence |
|---------|------------|
| **Expired certificate** | Browsers show a warning, users cannot access the service |
| **Stolen private key** | Attackers can impersonate the legitimate owner |
| **Compromised <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice>** | All certificates from that <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice> are untrustworthy |
| **Self-signed certificate** | No third party has verified the identity |

In 2011, the Dutch <Voice text="C.A.">CA</Voice> **DigiNotar** was compromised. Hackers issued fake certificates for Gmail, Yahoo, and others. The result? The entire company shut down.
