Imagine standing in a busy train station in London. You need to get from King's Cross to Heathrow. You open a map. And that map shows you exactly how far 27 kilometers is. It shows every twist in the tunnel. It shows that the Piccadilly line actually runs northeast for 3.2 kilometers before turning southwest.

**You would be lost in seconds.**

This was exactly the problem in 1931. The official London Underground map was a geographical nightmare. Stations in the center were crammed into an unreadable cluster. Outer stations were so far away they nearly fell off the paper.

Then came Harry Beck.

---

## The Man Who Wasn't a Cartographer

Harry Beck wasn't a mapmaker. He was an **electrical draftsman**. He worked on sketching circuit diagrams for the signaling systems on the London Underground.

And that background gave him the insight.

Beck realized something revolutionary: *Passengers underground don't care about what's happening above ground.* They only want to know:
- Which station comes next?
- Where do I change lines?
- How many stops to my destination?

Geographical distance? Irrelevant.  
Tunnel curves? Meaningless.  
The only thing that matters is **connections**.

## Two Worlds: Topographical vs. Topological

Beck invented (without knowing it) a visual concept now used everywhere:

| Type | Focus | Example |
|------|-------|---------|
| **Topographical** | Geography, distance, scale | Traditional road maps |
| **Topological** | Connections, sequence, structure | Metro maps, circuit diagrams |

A **topographical map** shows the world as it *is*.  
A **topological map** shows the world as it's *used*.

And that's the difference between getting lost and finding your way.

## Beck's Three Revolutionary Rules

Beck didn't just draw a map. He created a **design system** with strict constraints:

### Rule 1: Only Three Angles
All lines run either:
- Horizontal (0°)
- Vertical (90°)
- Diagonal (45°)

No arbitrary curves. No "almost straight" lines. Absolute geometric discipline.

### Rule 2: Equidistant Stations
On the map, every station appears to be the same distance from the next. In reality, there might be 200 meters or 4 kilometers between them. But on the map? Same visual distance.

Beck compressed the crowded center and expanded the sparse suburbs to give everything room to breathe.

### Rule 3: Connections Over Geography
Stations where multiple lines meet (King's Cross, Bank, Oxford Circus) were highlighted with large white circles. Regular stations got just a small tick mark.

## Rejected — Then Beloved

When Beck presented his map to London Underground's publicity department in 1931, it was rejected. "Too radical," they said. "People won't understand it."

Beck didn't give up. He worked on the design in his spare time. And in 1933, a limited edition was printed as a test.

It sold out in days.

Passengers loved it. For the first time, anyone could navigate London's complex network without being an expert. The map solved the problem by **removing useless information** and highlighting what was **useful**.

## The Legacy Lives On

Today, almost every metro system in the world uses a variation of Beck's technique:

- **New York** (though they fought against it for decades)
- **Tokyo** (with more lines than London, but the same principles)
- **Paris** (elegantly adapted to the city's circular shape)
- **Copenhagen** (even with just a few Metro lines)

But what if we took Beck's principles and applied them to something other than trains?

What if we mapped **business processes** like metro lines?
